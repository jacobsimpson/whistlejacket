Our Services

Whistle Jacket Training offers only the best in training and instruction for all horses and all riders. With over 20 years experience our goal is to provide each individual with a customized program to fit their needs.

Our program is about learning to listen, learning to be softer, learning to be mindful and thoughtful. This means taking things slowly and trying to understand things more from the horse’s perspective. Whether we’re doing ground work, riding, or training the goal is careful and methodical work where we strive to understand our horses and do our best for them.

The experience of horses is unlike any other in life as they offer us relationship, softness, beauty, simplicity, wrapped up in mystery and wildness and strength that touches something deep in the soul. And the amazing part is that horses are so desperate to communicate with us, but we have to be willing to listen and to take the time to let them teach us.

Boarding Full care $800 per month. Horses live in loafing sheds with attached gravel paddocks year round, and access to a track system or grass pastures to suit their needs. We use slow feeder systems and turnout space to provide food access 24/7, which takes away the stress at feeding time and encourages movement. Horses can share spaces, or live as individuals. Paddocks and turnout areas are cleaned daily and kept mud free through careful management. Property features a covered arena, covered round pen and trails on the 60 acre property for enjoyment year round.

Groundwork Lessons

The relationship with our horses is first established from the ground, where we have a profound impact. Spending time doing intentional exercises from the ground gives riders a chance to observe how their horse thinks and moves. For the horse these exercises engage the thinking side of their brain, rather than the reactive side, which helps them be more confident and relaxed which is a much better starting place for any riding.

Groundwork includes basic leading exercises, maneuverability exercises to move forehand and hindquarters, lateral work for suppling using leg-yield and shoulder-in, thoughtful exercises with obstacles like poles, barrels and cones, also lunging, and liberty work in the round pen.

Riding Instruction

Riding lessons are about equipping the rider’s mind and body to be able to better communicate and adapt with their horse as they forge a riding relationship together. First this is about understanding how the rider’s body affects the horse. This approach is focused primarily on the seat and taking the time needed to build a strong foundation- sometimes this means working on the lunge line, even without stirrups and reins. Second it is about body control through balance, movement and softness to communicate with their horse in a way that helps the horse understand. Horses always want us to be softer and quieter, more like a physical therapist than a boot camp sergeant!

School horses may be available for select riders.

Thoughtful Dressage Training

A training approach based in Classical dressage with a focus on helping horses be comfortable and thoughtful about their bodies and the work being asked of them. This means careful and methodical training that develops both mental willingness and physical balance and softness. Time and care is taken for each horse to meet their individual needs with no rushing; a well trained horse is a pleasure to ride for a lifetime, not just a few prime years. Training combines groundwork, lunging and riding sessions as appropriate for each horse and owner combination.

About Us

Whistle Jacket Training was founded in 2006 by Kim Holley although Kim’s passion or horses started long before that. Having started riding when she was 12, she gained much of her Classical dressage foundation from a long apprenticeship with an Arabian breeder who trained in the Classical style. Her experience with Arabians was wide and varied from breeding to starting young horses to preparing horses for the show ring and competing, but most of all she learned lightness from the highly sensitive and intelligent breed. Kim graduated in 2004 from Rocky Mt College in Billings, MT with a BS in Equestrian Business, and soon started her training business. Since then she has had opportunity to work with many horses and riders, do some competing, attend clinics and refine her skills and philosophy as a horse trainer. Kim enjoys pulling from a wide range of trainers including Classical Dressage masters like Podhajsky, Steinbrecht and DeKunffy, as well as Horsemanship trainers like Mark Rashid and Buck Brannaman, and has been greatly influenced by biomechanics specialists like Sally Swift, Mary Wanless and Gerd Heuschmann. Kim finds delight in the journey and the promise of growth in her students and horses, and enjoys being a part of their learning and progress.

Kim’s Philosophy

The most valuable tool we have with our horses is a relationship of trust and understanding. Horse have such deep souls and have so much to give, the journey is about diving into those depths and discovering what they have to teach us. Each horse and human relationship is unique so the aim is to explore what works for each individual.

We tend to ask so much of our horses expecting them to acclimate and engage in our human world in the ways we choose, but what if we approached each encounter with less of an agenda and more of a conversation- both talking and listening? Given time and opportunity I think we might find that our horses have so much to tell us, and are very interested in communicating!

A horse’s world is simple in many ways: they desire food and safety and comfort, and in each of these they thrive on consistency. This builds trust and through trust comes greater softness, willingness and even interest in the things we ask them to do. What seems to be the hardest part for us to understand though, is that this takes time. We often are in a hurry to get somewhere, or we’re task driven so we want to practice a skill again and again until we get it right. The challenge is that horses don’t see the world this way, and usually the more we try the worse things get. We have to be willing to slow down and let go of our timelines and agendas and be willing to work with the horse at their pace.

The more I work with horses the more I realize my own shortcomings but from that I gain a greater appreciation for what horses offer, despite my mistakes. And I strive to listen better, and to wait longer, and to adapt to what the horse needs as I ask them to adapt to what I desire, a partnership. The same is true of my approach to teaching people to ride or work with horses. Each individual brings their unique desires and needs to the experience and we journey together, listening and talking, working and resting, thinking and doing, however long it takes, doing our utmost to enjoy the time together along the way.
